# Chasing Kubernetes: Why Copying Your Competitor Isn’t a Strategy

Let’s be honest—there’s nothing quite like the cold sweat that comes over you when a board member leans in and asks, “Why aren’t we using Kubernetes like our competitors?” I’ve been in that room. The pressure is palpable. Suddenly, it feels as if your entire technology roadmap is up for grabs, not because it’s wrong, but because someone else is doing something different.

It’s a familiar story. Vendors are circling, case studies are flying about, and every tech blog seems to be singing the praises of the latest shiny thing. Kubernetes, microservices, AI—pick your buzzword. The message is clear: if you’re not on the bandwagon, you’re falling behind.

But let’s pause for a moment. Is that really strategy, or is it just peer pressure dressed up in a suit?

## The Boardroom Echo Chamber

I’ve seen it time and again. A competitor announces a big move—maybe they’re “all in” on Kubernetes, or they’ve just migrated everything to the cloud. Suddenly, your execs are asking, “Why aren’t we doing that?” It’s as if the mere act of *not* following suit is a sign of failure.

Here’s the thing: just because your competitor is doing something doesn’t mean it’s right for you. In fact, blindly copying others is a surefire way to end up with a Frankenstein’s monster of a tech stack—expensive, unwieldy, and not fit for purpose.

I remember working with a CTO who was under immense pressure to “modernise” their stack. The board wanted Kubernetes because, well, everyone else had it. But when we dug into their actual needs, it turned out their existing systems weren’t the bottleneck. The real issues were process-related—slow decision-making, unclear ownership, and a lack of visibility into what was actually slowing delivery. Kubernetes wouldn’t have solved any of that. In fact, it would have added complexity and cost.

## Evidence Over Emotion

This is where I see tech leaders really earn their stripes—not by chasing trends, but by pushing back with evidence. It’s not about being stubborn or resistant to change. It’s about being deliberate.

Here’s how we approach it:

- **Make your current systems visible.** You can’t improve what you can’t see. Map out your architecture, your workflows, your pain points. Get the facts on the table.
- **Identify what’s *actually* slowing you down.** Is it the tech, or is it the way you’re working? Nine times out of ten, it’s the latter.
- **Evaluate new tech based on fit, not hype.** Does it align with your business goals? Will your teams thrive with it, or will it just look good in a pitch deck?

I’ve lost count of the number of times a “must-have” technology turned out to be a distraction. One client was convinced they needed microservices because their competitor had them. But their product was simple, their team was small, and the overhead would have been crippling. We ran the numbers, looked at their actual needs, and decided to stick with a monolith—for now. The result? Faster delivery, happier teams, and a lot less stress.

## Walking Into the Room With Confidence

When you’ve done the work—when you’ve got clarity on what’s really needed—you can walk into any room and defend your decisions. Not because you’re being defensive, but because your choices are rooted in strategy, not reaction.

This is what real leadership looks like:

- **Confident, not reactive.**
- **Deliberate, not desperate.**
- **On your terms, not someone else’s.**

It’s not always easy. The pressure to conform is real. But the alternative—chasing every trend, second-guessing your roadmap, and ending up with a mess—serves no one.

## Practical Steps: How to Push Back (Productively)

If you’re feeling the heat, here’s what I recommend:

1. **Map your current state.** Get a clear, honest picture of your systems, processes, and pain points.
2. **Gather evidence.** Where are the real bottlenecks? What’s working, and what isn’t?
3. **Evaluate new tech against your actual needs.** Not just what’s trendy, but what will move the needle for your business.
4. **Communicate with clarity.** Bring your findings to the boardroom. Show your work. Explain your reasoning.
5. **Stay open, but stay grounded.** Be willing to adapt, but don’t be swayed by hype alone.

## Final Thoughts: Lead on Your Terms

At the end of the day, leadership isn’t about following the herd. It’s about making choices that serve your business, your teams, and your customers. That takes courage, discipline, and a willingness to say, “No, that’s not right for us—yet.”

So next time someone asks, “Why aren’t we doing what they’re doing?”—take a breath, pull out your evidence, and lead the conversation. That’s how you build a technology strategy you can stand behind.

**What’s driving your technology decisions right now? Are you leading with evidence, or reacting to pressure?** Take a hard look, and don’t be afraid to challenge the status quo. Your teams—and your sanity—will thank you.
