# Not Every Shiny Object Deserves Your Attention (or Your Budget)

Let’s be honest—there’s always a new shiny thing lighting up your inbox. It’s the latest framework, the AI-powered tool, the “game-changer” that everyone on LinkedIn is suddenly an expert in. Your team’s buzzing about it, your competitors are making noise, and before you know it, you’re left wondering: *Are we missing out if we don’t jump on this?*

I’ve been there. More times than I care to admit, actually. The pressure to keep up, to not be the one left behind, is real. But here’s the uncomfortable truth: chasing hype without discipline doesn’t just waste time—it saps your team’s energy, clutters your architecture, and, worst of all, erodes trust when the new thing doesn’t deliver.

## The Cost of Chasing Hype

Let me paint you a picture. Years ago, I worked with a team that was absolutely convinced that microservices were the answer to all their problems. Every conference talk, every blog post, every consultant seemed to be singing the same tune. So, we dove in headfirst.

What did we get? A spaghetti mess of services, a team exhausted by context-switching, and a product that was harder to ship than ever. The technology wasn’t the problem—the lack of rigour in our decision-making was. We never stopped to ask: *Is this right for us, right now?* We just didn’t want to be left behind.

Sound familiar?

## Building Discipline Into Tech Choices

This is where I see so many CTOs and tech leaders stumble. The temptation is to say yes to the new thing because it feels like progress. But good leadership isn’t about being first to every trend. It’s about knowing *why* you’re saying yes—or no.

Here’s what I’ve learned (sometimes the hard way):

- **Frameworks matter.** You need a structured way to evaluate new tech. Not just “can we do it?” but “*should* we do it, and is *now* the right time?”
- **ROI isn’t just a buzzword.** If you can’t articulate the return—whether it’s speed, quality, or customer value—then you’re gambling, not investing.
- **Strategic alignment is non-negotiable.** If the shiny thing doesn’t move you closer to your goals, it’s a distraction.
- **Team capability fit is critical.** Just because your competitor shipped it doesn’t mean your team is ready—or that it’s even relevant to your context.

## Making Emerging Tech Assessments Part of Your System

Here’s the pragmatic bit: you need to make tech evaluation a *system*, not an ad hoc reaction. That means:

- **Repeatable processes.** Don’t reinvent the wheel every time. Have a checklist, a rubric, a set of questions you always ask.
- **Defensible decisions.** Be able to explain, to your board or your team, *why* you made the call you did. “Because everyone else is doing it” doesn’t cut it.
- **Continuous learning.** Build in regular reviews. What worked? What didn’t? What did we learn?

I’ve seen teams transform when they stop reacting and start leading. The anxiety drops. The architecture gets cleaner. The team trusts the process—and each other.

## Real-World Example: Saying No (and Why It Mattered)

A few years back, a client of mine was under pressure to adopt a flashy new cloud service. The sales pitch was compelling, the demos were slick, and the competition was already boasting about their “digital transformation.” But when we ran it through our evaluation framework, it was clear: the cost outweighed the benefit, the team wasn’t ready, and the strategic fit just wasn’t there.

We said no. And you know what? Six months later, that “must-have” service was quietly sunsetted by the vendor. The team was relieved, the budget was intact, and trust in leadership was stronger than ever.

## Stop Reacting. Start Leading.

So, here’s my challenge to you:

- **What’s your process for evaluating new tech?**
- **Are you clear on your “why” for every yes and every no?**
- **Does your team trust that decisions are made with rigour, not just FOMO?**

If you’re not sure, now’s the time to build that discipline. Make emerging tech assessment part of your system of work—structured, repeatable, and defensible.

Because leadership isn’t about chasing every shiny object. It’s about knowing which ones matter, and having the courage to say no to the rest.
