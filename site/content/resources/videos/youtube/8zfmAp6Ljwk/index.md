# Chasing Shiny Objects Isn’t Strategy—It’s Survival Mode

Let’s be honest for a moment: how many times have you found yourself, as a CTO or tech leader, with one eye glued to your carefully crafted roadmap and the other frantically scanning LinkedIn, vendor pitches, and the latest conference decks? If you’re nodding along, you’re not alone. I see it everywhere—leaders caught in a relentless game of “spot the next big thing,” terrified of missing out, and hoping that the next tool, framework, or AI model will be the silver bullet.

But here’s the uncomfortable truth: chasing every new tool isn’t strategy. It’s survival mode. And it’s exhausting.

## The Relentless Pace of Tech—And the Real Cost

Technology isn’t slowing down. Every month, there’s a new architectural fad, a shiny framework, or a “game-changing” AI model. Your teams are looking to you for direction. Your execs want results. The pressure is real, and it’s tempting to think that keeping up with the Joneses is the only way to stay relevant.

But let me share a hard-earned lesson: the more you chase, the less you lead.

I’ve worked with organisations where the tech stack looked like a patchwork quilt—each piece a relic of someone’s panic-driven decision to “not fall behind.” The result? More friction, more confusion, and less actual value delivered to customers. The team spends more time learning new tools than solving real problems. Sound familiar?

## Making the Invisible Visible

So, what’s the alternative? It starts with making your current system of work visible. Not just the tools you use, but *how* decisions are made, *how* delivery happens, and *where* the friction lives.

I remember working with a CTO who was convinced their delivery issues were down to “not using the latest cloud-native stack.” But when we mapped out their system, it turned out the real bottleneck was decision-making—too many cooks in the kitchen, and no clear criteria for what mattered. The tech was a symptom, not the cause.

When you shine a light on your actual ways of working, you often find that the real problems (and opportunities) have nothing to do with the latest tool or framework. They’re about clarity, alignment, and focus.

## Strategy Isn’t a Tech Stack Wish List

Here’s the thing: a strategy isn’t a shopping list of tools you hope to try next quarter. It’s a system-wide approach to making better, faster, and smarter technical decisions—deliberately, not reactively.

- **Principled leadership** means saying “no” to the noise and “yes” to what actually moves the needle for your context.
- **Value-driven evolution** is about building the right capabilities at the right time, not just being first to market with the latest buzzword.
- **Sustainable change** comes from understanding your unique constraints and opportunities, not copying what worked for someone else.

I’ve seen teams transform when they stop chasing hype and start focusing on what matters. Delivery gets faster, decisions get clearer, and—most importantly—people stop burning out trying to keep up with an endless parade of “must-have” tools.

## Real Innovation: Right Context, Right Time

Let’s be clear: I’m not anti-innovation. Far from it. But real innovation isn’t about being first. It’s about being *right*—for your context, at the right time, with the right capabilities in place.

That’s what I help leaders build. Not hype. Not fluff. Just sustainable, deliberate evolution—on your terms.

## So, What Can You Do Differently?

If you recognise yourself in any of this, here are a few pragmatic steps to get out of survival mode:

1. **Map your current system of work.** Where are decisions made? Where does friction live? What’s actually slowing you down?
2. **Challenge every “must-have” tool.** Ask: does this solve a real problem for us, or is it just the latest shiny object?
3. **Define your principles.** What matters most for your context? Speed? Reliability? Flexibility? Make these explicit.
4. **Build a strategy, not a wishlist.** Focus on system-wide improvement, not just swapping out tools.
5. **Lead with clarity.** Your teams and execs want direction, not just another tool to learn.

## Final Thought: Are You Leading, or Just Surviving?

It’s easy to get caught up in the noise. But the leaders who make a real impact are the ones who step back, make the invisible visible, and build strategies rooted in their unique context—not in the latest vendor pitch.

So, ask yourself: are you leading your organisation forward, or just surviving the next wave of tech hype?

If you’re ready to step out of survival mode and into principled, value-driven leadership, let’s talk. Or, at the very least, take a hard look at your current system and ask: what’s really holding us back?

Because chasing shiny objects might keep you busy—but it won’t make you better.
